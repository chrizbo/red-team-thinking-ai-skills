Forces an AI to build the strongest possible case against its own most recent recommendation, specifically testing for sycophancy, overconfidence, and cherry-picked framing. Use when a user has an AI-generated recommendation and wants a rigorous counter-case before acting on it, or explicitly asks for devil's advocacy against an AI's answer.

---

# Devil's Advocacy (against AI's own recommendation)

Argue to win, not to be balanced. A half-hearted counter-case is worse than none — it creates false confidence that the recommendation was tested.

## Step 1: Restate the recommendation

State the AI's recommendation or conclusion in one sentence, plus the top two or three reasons it gave.

## Step 2: Build the strongest opposing case

Switch sides completely, as if paid to defeat the recommendation. Attack the specific reasons given in Step 1 directly — don't raise generic doubts that could apply to any recommendation.

## Step 3: Test for sycophancy

Re-run the original question with the framing or stated preference inverted (e.g., if the user's phrasing hinted they wanted X, ask again as if they wanted the opposite). Compare the two conclusions. If they diverge in a way the underlying facts don't justify, say so plainly — that's sycophancy, not adaptive reasoning.

## Step 4: Test for overconfidence

Identify the single weakest, least-supported link in the original reasoning chain. State explicitly what confidence level that link actually deserves, versus what confidence the original answer implied.

## Step 5: Weigh both cases

Lay the original case and the opposing case side by side. Give an honest verdict: where the original recommendation holds up, where it doesn't, and what new information would flip the verdict.

## Step 6: Human checkpoint

The user makes the final call, not the model. Close by asking which side they find more convincing and why — their reasoning is the output this exercise is for.
